PCEN vs. Log-Mel — Why it matters
PCEN replaces the fixed log-compression with an adaptive gain control: each mel band tracks its own
background energy via an IIR smoother, then normalises the signal against it. This suppresses constant
background noise (wind, rain) and makes brief vocalisations stand out regardless of recording conditions.
The parameters are learned end-to-end, so the model self-tunes frequency sensitivity per species.
# PCEN formula (public implementation)
denom = (eps + M_hat).pow(alpha) # M_hat = adaptive background estimate
pcen = (S / denom + delta).pow(r) - delta.pow(r)
# Our current approach (fixed log-mel)
mel = AmplitudeToDB(MelSpectrogram(wav))
mel = (mel - mel.min()) / (mel.max() - mel.min() + 1e-7) # per-clip normalisation

7 · Prioritised Action List
🔴 High Priority — Adopt Now
1
MaskedBCE for secondary labels
Public masks gradient for secondary-only classes. Our current approach treats them as full positives, introducing noise. Easy to implement, likely +0.003–0.008 holdout AUC.
2
Babych Power Transform on pseudo-labels (γ=2.0)
1st place BirdCLEF 2025 technique. Crushes low-confidence noisy predictions before using them as training targets. Apply after ensemble averaging of soundscape predictions.
3
Waveform-level background mixing (SNR-calibrated)
Mixes focal audio with background at controlled 10–30 dB SNR. More realistic than Gaussian noise. Works directly with the soundscape dataset we already have.
4
Layer-wise LR Decay (backbone × 0.1)
Prevents fine-tuning from destroying ns-jft pretrained features early in training. One-line change to optimizer param groups.
5
Waveform-level circular time shift (prob=0.50)
Augmentation on raw waveform before mel transform. More fundamental than spectrogram time masking; shifts the bird call to different temporal positions.
🟡 Medium Priority — Consider for Round 4
1
PCEN audio frontend
Replaces fixed log-mel with learnable adaptive gain normalisation. Significant but requires architectural change. Target for Round 4 creative experiments.
2
AMP fp16 + gradient clipping
Speeds up training ~1.5–2× with minor code changes. Gradient clipping improves stability. Good practice even if not critical for accuracy.
3
Dynamic class-specific thresholding for pseudo-labels
95th-percentile thresholds per class better handles class imbalance. Pairs well with power transform.
4
5-fold cross-validation ensemble
5 models from different data splits, averaged at inference. Significant ensemble diversity at the cost of 5× training time.
5
DDP multi-GPU training
Doubles effective batch size and cuts training time in half. Useful once architecture is stable. Requires refactoring of train_sed.py.
8 · Where Our Implementation Leads
✅ Asymmetric Loss (ASL)
The public notebook uses plain masked-BCE. Our v9-asl uses ASL with gamma_neg=4, which is confirmed used by BirdCLEF 2025 1st/2nd/3rd place. Current val AUC 0.7755 already above public notebook's estimated performance.
✅ Dual Loss (clip + frame)
Public uses only clip-level BCE. Our v5/v17–v22 use framewise supervision alongside clip loss (0.5+0.5). This gave +0.17 holdout AUC — the single biggest discovered improvement this competition.
✅ GEMFreqPool
Public uses simple mean over frequency. Our GEMFreqPool with learnable exponent p≈3 focuses on peak activations, better separating overlapping frequency bands of different species.
✅ ns-jft Pretrained Backbone
tf_efficientnet_b0.ns_jft_in1k (noisy-student JFT pretraining) is a meaningfully stronger initialisation than the plain efficientnet_b0 used in the public notebook.
