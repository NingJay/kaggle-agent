1. Competition Overview
BirdCLEF+ 2026 is a Kaggle code competition focused on passive acoustic monitoring (PAM) of wildlife in the
Brazilian Pantanal — the world's largest tropical wetland (150,000+ km²), home to 650+ bird
species and countless other fauna. A growing network of 1,000 acoustic recorders generates continuous audio
that is too large to review manually. The goal is to build a machine learning system that detects and classifies
wildlife species from 5-second audio windows.

Task Description
For each 5-second window of every test soundscape, predict the probability of each of the 234 species being
present. The submission is a CSV with one row per window and 234 probability columns.
The evaluation metric is macro-averaged ROC-AUC (skipping classes with no true positives in test).
Key Constraints
CPU-only inference — GPU notebooks are disabled (1 min runtime). All inference must run on CPU in 90 min.
No internet access during submission — models must be pre-packaged in the notebook.
Freely available external data and pre-trained models are allowed.
Submission notebook: 90 min CPU runtime max.

Data Sources
Training audio comes from two sources:
Xeno-canto (XC) — community recordings of bird vocalizations, with quality ratings 1–5
iNaturalist (iNat) — citizen science recordings, typically unrated
Additionally, expert-labeled train_soundscapes from the actual Pantanal recording sites provide in-domain data.

2.4 Key Data Challenges Summary
Challenge 1: Severe Class Imbalance
97.9% of training recordings are birds. Non-bird species (Amphibia, Insecta, Mammalia, Reptilia) are severely
underrepresented. The lone Reptilia species has exactly 1 training recording.
Challenge 2: Few-Shot Species
25 species have fewer than 10 training recordings. 18 species have ≤5 recordings.
These require zero/few-shot learning techniques.
Challenge 3: Domain Shift
Training data is short, focal recordings of individual animals (XC/iNat).
Test data is continuous passive recordings of entire soundscapes with overlapping species and background noise.
This is the #1 performance bottleneck historically.
Challenge 4: Multi-Label Detection
89.4% of soundscape segments contain multiple simultaneous species.
Models must do multi-label classification, not single-label.
Challenge 5: CPU-Only Inference Constraint
~600 one-minute soundscapes must be processed in 90 minutes on CPU.
That is ~600 × 12 = 7,200 five-second segments. Heavy models will not fit.
Inference speed is as critical as model accuracy.
Challenge 6: Unlabeled Soundscape Data
Additional train_soundscapes without labels are available — a huge opportunity for
semi-supervised learning and pseudo-labeling.

4. Recommended Approaches for 2026
4.1 Foundation: Audio Feature Extraction
HIGH PRIORITY Mel-Spectrogram CNN (Proven Baseline)
Convert audio to Mel-spectrogram images and apply 2D CNNs. This is the universal foundation of all top solutions.
Settings: 32kHz sample rate, n_mels=128, hop_length=320 (10ms), window 5–10 seconds
Augmentation: Time masking, frequency masking, MixUp, FilterAugment, gain jitter, Gaussian noise
librosa torchaudio albumentations
MEDIUM PRIORITY Pre-trained Bioacoustic Embeddings
Use Google Perch or BirdNET as feature extractors, fine-tuned on competition data.
Perch (Google): Multi-species audio embedding model, state of the art; use TFLite for fast CPU inference
BirdNET : Specialized bird call classifier; strong prior BirdCLEF performance
BirdSetEfficientNetB1 : Best single pre-trained model in 2025 (public 0.810)
TFLite ONNX Google Perch
4.2 Model Architecture
HIGH PRIORITY EfficientNet Family
The dominant architecture across all recent BirdCLEF competitions. Excellent accuracy/speed tradeoff for CPU inference.
EfficientNetV2-S (tf_efficientnetv2_s_in21k) — strong baseline, used by 2nd place 2025
EfficientNet-B0/B3/B4 — lightweight options for ensemble diversity
ECA-NfNet-L0 — alternative architecture for ensemble diversity
timm PyTorch
MEDIUM PRIORITY Sound Event Detection (SED) Models
Frame-level prediction within each clip; particularly useful for detecting species at specific moments in soundscapes.
Complement CNN classifiers with SED for better temporal localization.
4.3 Training Strategy
HIGH PRIORITY Multi-Stage Pretraining
Stage 1 : Pretrain on all historical BirdCLEF data (2021–2025) — proven +1–2% AUC improvement
Stage 2 : Fine-tune on 2026 training data (train.csv + soundscape labels)
Stage 3 : Semi-supervised training using pseudo-labeled unlabeled soundscapes
HIGH PRIORITY Semi-Supervised Learning / Pseudo-Labeling
Train initial models → predict on unlabeled train_soundscapes → use confident predictions as additional training labels → retrain.
Multiple rounds of pseudo-labeling progressively improve domain adaptation.
Use threshold ≥ 0.7–0.8 confidence for pseudo-label inclusion
Apply power scaling to soft pseudo-labels
Combine with MixUp for regularization
HIGH PRIORITY Train 10s → Infer 5s Strategy
Train models on 10-second audio windows (more context, more data diversity) but inference on 5-second windows to match test format.
This consistently improves performance across multiple years.
MEDIUM PRIORITY Loss Function: SoftAUCLoss
Optimize directly for the evaluation metric (AUC) rather than cross-entropy. SoftAUCLoss is resistant to overfitting while supporting soft labels from pseudo-labeling. Combine with BCE for stability.
4.4 Handling Non-Bird Species (Key 2026 Challenge)
HIGH PRIORITY Specialized Non-Bird Sub-Models
Given the extreme imbalance (Reptilia: 1 recording!), specialized handling is essential:
Train separate or weighted models for Amphibia, Insecta, Mammalia, Reptilia
Leverage labeled soundscapes heavily — they contain in-domain non-bird calls
Use different augmentation strategies: non-bird calls have very different spectral properties
Consider oversampling non-bird species during training (weighted sampling)
For the single Reptilia recording: treat as extreme few-shot; use embedding similarity
MEDIUM PRIORITY External Data for Non-Birds
Augment rare non-bird species with external recordings:
iNaturalist additional recordings for amphibians/reptiles in South America
FrogID / AnuranSet datasets for amphibian calls
DCASE datasets for insect sound events
4.5 Ensemble Strategy
HIGH PRIORITY Quantile-Mix Ensembling
Blend multiple model predictions using Quantile-Mix (α=0.5): combine mean averaging and rank averaging of predictions.
Recommended ensemble composition:
2–3 EfficientNet variants (different sizes for diversity)
1 Perch/BirdNET-based model
1 SED model
⚠️ CPU Inference Constraint: Each additional model in the ensemble adds inference time.
Profile inference speed carefully. Use TFLite/ONNX conversion to maximize speed.
4.6 Post-Processing
MEDIUM PRIORITY Score Calibration & Species Prior
Apply species-specific thresholds learned from validation soundscapes
Use temporal smoothing: if species detected in adjacent 5-second windows, boost confidence
Apply geographic/habitat priors: Pantanal species lists from literature
Power scaling of predictions: raise scores to power p (p < 1) to spread probability mass

6. Inference Optimization Strategy
The 90-minute CPU-only constraint is the defining technical challenge of BirdCLEF+ 2026. Getting this right is essential — a better model that times out scores zero.
Techniques to Fit Within 90 Minutes
Technique Speedup Tradeoff
ONNX Runtime export 2–4x vs PyTorch Minimal accuracy loss
TFLite export ~10x for Perch Model-dependent
OpenVINO 2–3x on Intel CPUs Platform-specific
Batch inference (large batches) 2–5x vs single More RAM usage
FP16 / INT8 quantization 1.5–3x Small accuracy drop
Smaller model (EfficientNet-B0) 3–4x vs B4 Less accurate
Parallel segment processing Nx (N cores) CPU memory bound
Pre-compute all spectrograms Avoid recompute RAM usage
Recommended Inference Strategy
Convert all models to ONNX. Use ONNX Runtime with multi-threading enabled.
Process all 600 soundscapes in batches. Budget ~8 seconds per soundscape (90 min / 600 files = 9 sec/file with margin).
Profile on CPU before finalizing ensemble size. Start with 2 models and add more only if time budget allows.
7. Key Insights & Conclusions
Insight 1: Non-Bird Species Are the Differentiator
All competitors will get birds right. The leaderboard positions will be determined by how well models
handle Amphibia (35 species, 451 recordings), Insecta (28 species, 199 recordings), Mammalia (8 species, 99 recordings),
and critically Reptilia (1 species, 1 recording). These are the hardest and most differentiating targets.
Invest heavily in non-bird species handling.
Insight 2: Labeled Soundscapes Are Gold
The 66 labeled soundscape files with 1,478 segments are from the same recording sites as test data.
They represent in-domain, in-distribution data with expert annotations. Some species only appear here.
Use these as: (a) primary fine-tuning data, (b) validation set for post-processing calibration,
(c) domain adaptation source. These are more valuable per-sample than XC data.
Insight 3: CPU Inference is the Real Bottleneck
Many Kaggle competitors fail to optimize for CPU inference and waste their accuracy advantage because
their notebooks time out. Convert to ONNX/TFLite early in development. Profile constantly.
A 0.92 AUC model that finishes in time beats a 0.95 AUC model that times out.
Insight 4: Pretraining on Prior BirdCLEF Data Is Essential
Every top 2025 solution used pretraining on 2021–2024 BirdCLEF data. This consistently adds +1–2% AUC.
The Pantanal overlaps with South American species covered in prior competitions.
This is a free performance boost — do this first.
Insight 5: Pseudo-Labeling for Domain Adaptation
The biggest performance gap is between focal recordings (training) and passive soundscapes (test).
Semi-supervised pseudo-labeling of unlabeled train_soundscapes directly addresses this.
Multiple rounds with increasing confidence thresholds progressively improve domain alignment.
Insight 6: Multi-Label Training is Non-Negotiable
89.4% of soundscape segments contain multiple species simultaneously.
Binary cross-entropy with sigmoid (not softmax) for multi-label is mandatory.
SoftAUCLoss with multi-label support directly optimizes for the evaluation metric.
Insight 7: Quality Over Quantity for External Data
The 2025 winner analysis showed that large volumes of extra external data can hurt performance.
Focus on geographic relevance (South America, Brazil specifically) and use quality ratings from Xeno-canto
to filter or weight training samples. The labeled soundscapes are higher quality than most XC data.

Dawn Chorus & Dusk Activity
The Pantanal (UTC−4) has peak bird activity at dawn (05:00–09:00 local) and dusk (17:00–21:00 local). Stratifying training by time-of-day — or adding hour-of-day as an auxiliary feature — can improve detection of time-specific vocalisers such as nocturnal frogs and insects.
Site-Level Analysis
Knowing which recording site each soundscape comes from allows site-specific post-processing (e.g. site-level species prior probabilities). Sites with more labeled data can inform pseudo-label confidence thresholds for unlabeled files from the same site.

CB-Loss: Effective Number of Samples
Class-Balanced Loss (Cui et al. 2019) uses the effective number of samples E(n) = (1 − β^n)/(1 − β)
with β = 0.9999. The resulting weight ratio of 487× between the rarest and most common

Top 10 Actionable Modeling Insights
1. Use soundscape labels as primary fine-tune data
They are expert-annotated, in-domain, from the exact recording sites as the test set.
2. 28 species exist ONLY in soundscape labels
These have zero train_audio recordings. Handle with embedding similarity or prototype networks on the soundscape segments.
3. Pretrain on BirdCLEF 2021–2025 before fine-tuning on 2026
All top 2025 solutions did this. Free +1–2% AUC improvement.
4. Class weight ratio is 0× — address imbalance explicitly
Use CB-Loss (β=0.9999), balanced sampling, or SoftAUCLoss. Training without balancing will fail on rare species.
5. Only 4.1% of training recs are within 500 km of Pantanal
Apply geographic filtering or proximity-based sample weighting during fine-tuning. Recs far from Pantanal may introduce harmful noise.
6. Convert to ONNX/TFLite before testing inference speed
The 90-min CPU limit is hard. A great model that times out scores zero.
7. Pseudo-label unlabeled soundscapes
Train model → predict unlabeled soundscapes → filter high-confidence → retrain. Bridges the focal-recording / passive-soundscape domain gap.
8. Train on 10-second windows, infer on 5-second windows
Consistent with top solutions in 2024 and 2025. More context during training improves representations.
9. 89.4% of soundscape windows are multi-species
Use sigmoid + BCE for multi-label output. Never softmax. Add temporal smoothing across adjacent windows at inference.
10. Exploit species co-occurrence as a post-processing prior
The top pair (Dwarf Tree Frog + Chaco Tree Frog) co-occurs in 382 windows. If A is detected with confidence, slightly boost B's score.
